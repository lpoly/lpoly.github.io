Optimizing the Trajectory of a Robotic Arm

Python Control Theory Simulation Optimization

Brief: This project focuses on optimizing the trajectory of a robotic arm in both 2D and 3D space. By leveraging forward kinematics and gradient-based optimization, the goal is to guide the arm‚Äôs end-effector toward a target point with minimal error. Implemented in Python, the system models joint behavior and applies numerical techniques to find optimal joint angles through simulation and cost minimization.

Details

In the 2D setup, we modeled a two-link robotic arm where the position of the end-effector was derived using forward kinematics. The goal was to minimize the Euclidean distance between the arm's hand and a desired target point by adjusting the joint angles using gradient-based optimization. We derived and implemented analytical gradients of the cost function with respect to each joint angle.

For the 3D extension, the robotic arm consists of three segments, each represented by two angles: azimuth (Œ∏) and elevation (œï). The arm's movement was modeled using spherical coordinates to capture the spatial orientation of each joint. This allowed for smooth and realistic simulation of the arm's trajectory in three dimensions.

A key challenge was determining the optimal step size during gradient descent. To address this, we implemented the Armijo backtracking line search method. This approach dynamically adjusts the step size based on a sufficient decrease condition, ensuring fast convergence while preventing overshooting and oscillations of the arm.

For visualizing the movement and optimization process, we used VPython to animate the robotic arm in 3D space. This provided an intuitive representation of how the arm adjusts its joints in real-time to reach the target, making the results both interpretable and visually engaging.

Among the optimization techniques tested, Nesterov Accelerated Gradient Descent (NAGD) consistently outperformed traditional Gradient Descent in both speed and convergence quality. The results were verified using numerical metrics and visual animation.
